Continuous AI

The Anti-Lock Brakes of Productivity

Generative AI means things that were always possible at a small scale now become practical to automate at a massive scale. Sometimes a change in scale is a change in principle. The question isn't how to get users from 10 to 20 AI queries a day—it's how to build for a world where AI reasons continuously, every few hundred milliseconds, across all modalities.

This is an argument for working backwards from the future of always-on AI rather than incrementally grinding forward from today's interaction patterns.

Key Ideas

Scale Changes Principle

What's possible at small scale becomes practical at massive scale. AI doesn't just do more—it changes what's worth doing.

Continuous Reasoning

Work back from a future where AI reasons every 100ms across all modalities, not from incrementally improving 10→20 queries/day.

Anti-Lock Brakes Metaphor

Once sensors and actuators became continuous, driving became easier—it removed the need for a specific skill set.

Skill Removal

Continuous AI removes the "skill" of productivity management itself. You don't need to practice braking in difficult circumstances.

The Anti-Lock Brakes Analogy

Before anti-lock braking systems, stopping safely on ice or in rain required genuine skill. Drivers had to learn "threshold braking"—applying pressure just below the point where wheels lock up, repeatedly pumping the pedal. It took practice. It required staying calm under pressure. Many people never mastered it.

Then ABS arrived. Sensors monitored wheel speed continuously. Actuators modulated brake pressure hundreds of times per second. The system reacted faster than any human could. Suddenly, the skill didn't matter. Even in harsh conditions, you could drive with confidence. The continuous sensing and acting eliminated the need for human expertise.

This is the trajectory for AI-assisted productivity. Today, being productive requires genuine skill—prioritization, time management, knowing what to ignore, remembering follow-ups. These are learnable but effortful. When AI reasons continuously across your calendar, email, tasks, and context, it can handle the "braking" for you. The skill of productivity management becomes as obsolete as threshold braking.

Working Backwards from the Future

The mistake is to start from where we are and inch forward. Right now, even a highly engaged user runs maybe 10-ish queries a day with Copilot. The incrementalist asks: "How do we get them to 20? To 50?" This is the wrong question.

The right question is: "What does a world look like where AI reasons every few hundred milliseconds, across all modalities?" Start there. Work backwards. Design for that world, not for marginal improvements to today's interaction patterns.

Removing Friction Everywhere

Consider planning a trip to India. With a human assistant like Mykaela, you provide: travel dates, starting point (LA, not Seattle), goals, and rough time allocation. Three days later, you board a plane with a binder containing every detail organized— flights, hotels, activities, reservations, contingencies.

What would it take to replicate that with AI, at similar levels of precision and personalization? The answer isn't "better prompts" or "more queries." It's continuous reasoning: AI that knows your preferences, monitors availability, tracks price changes, anticipates conflicts, and assembles the binder without being asked step-by-step.

This is the anti-lock brakes of travel planning. The skill of being an excellent trip organizer—knowing which sites to check, when to book, how to sequence activities—becomes unnecessary. The system handles it continuously.

What This Means

Design for Continuous, Not Discrete

Stop building for "queries" and start building for streams. AI should be monitoring, not waiting to be asked.

Skill Becomes Optional

Productivity management, like threshold braking, becomes a legacy skill. You can still learn it, but you don't need to.

Confidence in Harsh Conditions

When things get chaotic—too many emails, competing priorities, unexpected changes—the system handles it. You don't need to practice for the hard cases.

Trivial Economics

Continuous AI is already affordable. The bottleneck isn't compute cost—it's building the right abstractions and interfaces.

The Shift

We're at an inflection point similar to the introduction of ABS. The technology exists. The economics work. The question is whether we'll design systems that assume continuous AI reasoning, or keep building for a world of discrete queries and manual skill.

Work backwards from the future. Build for the world where AI reasons every few hundred milliseconds. That's where we're headed—and the sooner we design for it, the sooner productivity management becomes as effortless as braking in the rain.
